Started playing Minecraft in Japanese this week. The menus were mostly manageable since I already know the game inside out, but a handful of words stopped me cold enough to look them up.

<VocabCard
  word="松明"
  reading="たいまつ"
  definition="Torch; a burning stick used as a light source."
  example="洞窟の中に{松明}を置きました。"
  translation="I placed a torch inside the cave."
  context="Literally 'pine-bright' — 松 (pine) + 明 (bright). A poetic name for something so mundane. Makes more sense if you picture an actual burning pine branch rather than a block on a wall."
/>

<VocabCard
  word="洞窟"
  reading="どうくつ"
  definition="Cave; a natural underground hollow or tunnel."
  example="深い{洞窟}でダイヤモンドを見つけました！"
  translation="I found a diamond in a deep cave!"
  context="Both kanji mean roughly 'hollow/cavern' — 洞 and 窟 reinforce each other. You'll also see 洞穴 (ほらあな) for cave, but 洞窟 feels more dramatic, which suits Minecraft perfectly."
/>

<VocabCard
  word="村人"
  reading="むらびと"
  definition="Villager; an inhabitant of a village."
  example="{村人}から本を買いました。"
  translation="I bought a book from a villager."
  context="村 (village) + 人 (person) — transparent and logical. Note that 村 is むら on its own, but the reading shifts slightly when compounded. You'll see 村 constantly in RPGs whenever there's a town to visit."
/>

<VocabCard
  word="採掘"
  reading="さいくつ"
  definition="Mining; excavating ore or resources from the ground."
  example="夜は危険なので、地下で{採掘}しています。"
  translation="Since it's dangerous at night, I'm mining underground."
  context="採 means 'to gather/pick' and 掘 means 'to dig.' Together they specifically mean digging resources out of the earth. Verb form is 採掘する. You'll also hear just 掘る (ほる, to dig) used more casually."
/>

Then the animals started showing up:

<VocabCard
  word="羊"
  reading="ひつじ"
  definition="Sheep; a woolly domesticated mammal."
  example="{羊}から羊毛を集めました。"
  translation="I collected wool from the sheep."
  context="羊 also appears in 羊毛 (ようもう, wool) — which you'll see in the crafting menu. In compounds, 羊 often reads as よう instead of ひつじ, so keep an ear out for both."
/>

<VocabCard
  word="牛"
  reading="うし"
  definition="Cow; a large domesticated bovine animal."
  example="{牛}を倒して革を手に入れました。"
  translation="I defeated a cow and got leather."
  context="牛 is うし alone, but shifts to ぎゅう in compounds: 牛肉 (ぎゅうにく, beef), 牛乳 (ぎゅうにゅう, milk), 牛丼 (ぎゅうどん). A classic on'yomi/kun'yomi split worth memorizing."
/>

<VocabCard
  word="豚"
  reading="ぶた"
  definition="Pig; a pink domesticated mammal."
  example="{豚}に乗るには、ニンジンが要ります。"
  translation="To ride a pig, you need a carrot."
  context="Like 牛, the reading shifts in compounds: 豚肉 (ぶたにく, pork). The kanji is built from 月 (the meat radical) and 豕 (pig) — so literally 'meat pig,' which is blunt but honest."
/>

<VocabCard
  word="逃げる"
  reading="にげる"
  definition="To run away; to flee or escape."
  example="クリーパーを見たら、すぐ{逃げて}ください！"
  translation="If you see a creeper, please run away immediately!"
  context="An ichidan (ru-verb): drop the る and attach endings directly — 逃げます, 逃げて, 逃げない. Compare to the godan 走る (はしる, to run), which looks like a ru-verb but isn't: 走ります, 走って, 走らない."
/>
